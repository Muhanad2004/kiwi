### The 7 Steps

**① Topic Sentence**
What it does: States your claim + names the work + names the literary school(s) + states the central idea you will argue.
Formula: `In "[Work]," [Author] uses [technique/school] to show that [central idea].`

**② Explanation of Topic Sentence**
What it does: Unpacks your claim in more detail. Explains what the literary school means and how it connects to the central idea. No quote yet.

**③ Paving the Way for an Example**
What it does: A transition sentence that introduces where in the text the example comes from. Names the moment or character without quoting yet.
Formula: `[Author] develops this idea when [character/narrator] [does/thinks/says something].`

**④ Giving an Example**
What it does: The actual quote from the text. Use quotation marks. Attribute it correctly.
Note: Keep quotes precise. In an exam, if you can't remember the exact words, paraphrase closely and note it is from the text.

**⑤ Explaining and Analyzing the Example**
What it does: The biggest block. Explains what the quote means, why it matters, how it connects to the literary school, and what it reveals about theme.
This step is where most of the marks live. Do not just paraphrase the quote — analyze it.

**⑥ Showing How the Example Supports the Topic Sentence**
What it does: A "therefore" sentence that connects the analyzed example back to your original claim. Start with "Therefore" or "This passage shows" or "Because of this."

**⑦ Concluding Statement**
What it does: Closes the paragraph with a broader statement about what the work achieves. Pulls back from the specific example to the larger literary/thematic picture.

## Page 2: Applied — To Build a Fire

A complete model paragraph written specifically for "To Build a Fire," ready to adapt for the exam.

**Topic:** Naturalism and Determinism in "To Build a Fire"

**Complete paragraph:**

> ① In "To Build a Fire," Jack London uses Naturalism and Determinism to show that human beings are powerless against the forces of nature, and that no amount of intelligence or effort can overcome what the environment has already decided.

> ② Through Naturalism, London portrays the man not as a hero but as a biological creature operating within an indifferent natural system. Determinism adds to this by suggesting that the man's death was inevitable from the moment he chose to travel alone in extreme cold. No decision he made could have changed the outcome because the forces against him were simply too large.

> ③ London makes this most clear in the way the man's body fails him despite his desperate attempts to save himself.

> ④ As the man tries to light a fire under the snow-covered tree, the narrative describes how "the blood was alive in him, but the tips of all his fingers were dead" — his body is already shutting down beyond his control or understanding.

> ⑤ This moment illustrates the core Naturalist idea that the human body is subject to physical laws the mind cannot override. The man is intelligent — he knows he needs fire, he knows what to do — but because the cold is a force of nature and not a human opponent, his knowledge is useless. His hands cannot grip. His plan cannot execute. Determinism operates here because the conditions of the environment sealed his fate before the story even began. London's precise, clinical description of the body's failure (rather than an emotional, dramatic description) reflects the Naturalist method: nature is observed like a scientist would observe it, without sentiment.

> ⑥ Therefore, this passage demonstrates how London uses Naturalism's insistence on environmental power and Determinism's logic of inevitable outcomes to show that the man never had a real chance — because the decision had already been made by forces he could not control.

> ⑦ Through the man's failure and the dog's survival, London ultimately argues that nature operates by its own laws, that instinct is more reliable than intellect in that system, and that the universe has no interest in whether any individual creature lives or dies.

---

**Annotation notes (shown alongside the paragraph):**

- Note how the topic sentence names both Naturalism AND Determinism — this matches what the instructor values based on the model paragraph
- Note how step ⑤ is the longest block — this is where the marks live
- Note how the dog is mentioned in ⑦ even though the example in ④ is about the man — you can bring in other elements of the work in the conclusion to broaden the argument
- The quote in ④ is approximate for exam purposes — the key is accuracy of meaning, not verbatim perfection

## Page 3: Structure Cheat Sheet

A quick-scan card showing the 7 steps in minimal format — for last-minute review before entering the exam hall.

```
① TOPIC SENTENCE
   → Claim + Work + School + Central idea

② EXPLAIN YOUR CLAIM
   → What does the school mean here? How does it connect?

③ INTRODUCE THE EXAMPLE
   → Set up where in the text the example comes from

④ GIVE THE QUOTE
   → Exact or close-paraphrase, in quotation marks

⑤ ANALYZE THE QUOTE  ← most marks here
   → What does it mean? Why does it matter? How does it prove the school?

⑥ LINK BACK TO CLAIM
   → "Therefore / This shows / Because of this..."

⑦ CONCLUDE BROADLY
   → What does the work achieve overall? Pull back from the specific
```

Visual style: clean card, hunter green numbered circles for each step, DM Sans, champagne bg.
