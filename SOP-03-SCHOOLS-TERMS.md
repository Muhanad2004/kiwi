### SCHOOL 1: Naturalism

**Emoji:** 🌿  
**Period:** 1880s–1910s  
**One-line:** Literature that treats humans like animals — products of biology, environment, and forces they cannot control.

**Core beliefs:**
- Humans do not have free will — they are shaped entirely by heredity and environment
- Nature is indifferent — it does not care whether humans survive or suffer
- Science and observation should drive storytelling, not romance or idealism
- Life is often brutal, and literature should not flinch from showing that

**Key characteristics (how to spot it):**
- Characters who are controlled by forces larger than themselves
- Detailed, clinical, almost scientific descriptions of environment
- No divine justice — bad things happen to people regardless of morality
- The natural world is powerful; humans are small
- Themes of survival, instinct, poverty, determinism

**Works in this course:**
- "To Build a Fire" (Jack London) — primary Naturalist text
- "The Open Boat" (Stephen Crane) — Naturalism + Impressionism

**Authors:**
- Jack London — Naturalism, survival narratives, the American wilderness
- Stephen Crane — Naturalism + Impressionism, psychological depth

**Exam trigger phrases:**
- "indifferent to human suffering"
- "forces beyond human control"
- "survival of the fittest"
- "environment shapes the character"
- "biological determinism"

### SCHOOL 2: Impressionism

**Emoji:** 🎨  
**Period:** 1890s–1910s  
**One-line:** Literature that captures experience through subjective perception — how things feel and look to the characters, not how they objectively are.

**Core beliefs:**
- Reality is filtered through individual perception — two people see the same event differently
- Emotion, mood, and sensory experience matter more than plot mechanics
- The inner life of a character is as important as what happens externally
- Truth is not fixed — it shifts depending on who is perceiving it

**Key characteristics (how to spot it):**
- Vivid sensory descriptions (what is seen, heard, felt in the moment)
- Shifting emotional states — the character's mood affects how we experience the scene
- Fragmented or non-linear perception
- Focus on the immediate, the fleeting, the sensory
- Reader is placed inside the character's experience

**Works in this course:**
- "The Open Boat" (Stephen Crane) — especially the correspondent's perspective

**Authors:**
- Stephen Crane — the only Impressionist in this exam

**Exam trigger phrases:**
- "subjective perception"
- "shifting emotions"
- "sensory detail"
- "how the character perceives"
- "psychological realism"

**Important distinction — Naturalism vs Impressionism in The Open Boat:**
- Naturalism: the sea is a force that doesn't care if the men die
- Impressionism: we experience the sea through the correspondent's feelings and senses
- Crane uses BOTH — this is the key insight the model paragraph demonstrates

### SCHOOL 3: Realism

**Emoji:** 🪟  
**Period:** 1865–1900s  
**One-line:** Literature that depicts ordinary life as it actually is, without romanticizing or idealizing it.

**Core beliefs:**
- Literature should reflect real life — real people, real problems, real places
- Avoid the idealized heroes and dramatic plots of Romanticism
- Focus on middle and lower class experience
- Accurate observation of human behavior and social conditions

**Key characteristics (how to spot it):**
- Ordinary characters in ordinary situations
- Accurate dialogue (people talk like real people)
- Social commentary on class, race, gender, economy
- No miraculous endings or divine intervention
- Psychological depth — characters are complex and contradicted

**Works in this course:**
- "Richard Cory" (Robinson)
- "The Mill" (Robinson)
- "Doc Hill" and "Abel Melveny" (Masters)
- Mark Twain's "A True Story" (Week 5 — context only)

**Authors:**
- Edwin Arlington Robinson — Realism + Modernism
- Edgar Lee Masters — Realism + Modernism
- Mark Twain — Realism, Regionalism

**Exam trigger phrases:**
- "ordinary life"
- "social conditions"
- "class commentary"
- "psychological complexity"
- "anti-romantic"

### SCHOOL 4: Modernism

**Emoji:** ⚡  
**Period:** 1900s–1940s  
**One-line:** Literature that breaks traditional forms and questions whether meaning, order, or progress are even real.

**Core beliefs:**
- Traditional structures (rhyme, plot, heroism) are inadequate for modern experience
- WWI and industrialization destroyed faith in progress and order
- The individual is isolated — modern life fragments identity
- Experimentation with form is itself meaningful
- Consciousness and interiority matter more than external events

**Key characteristics (how to spot it):**
- Free verse or unconventional poetic form
- Fragmented narrative or non-linear structure
- Themes of alienation, isolation, disillusionment
- Rejection of traditional heroism or happy endings
- Ambiguity — nothing is resolved neatly

**Works in this course:**
- "Doc Hill" (Masters) — Modernist free verse, dramatic monologue
- "Abel Melveny" (Masters) — Modernist critique of materialism
- "Richard Cory" (Robinson) — Modernist irony, disillusionment
- "The Mill" (Robinson) — Modernist restraint, industrial despair

**Authors:**
- Edgar Lee Masters — Spoon River Anthology (1915)
- Edwin Arlington Robinson — early American Modernist

**Exam trigger phrases:**
- "disillusionment"
- "alienation"
- "fragmented identity"
- "rejection of traditional form"
- "free verse"
- "dramatic monologue"

### SCHOOL 5: Regionalism

**Emoji:** 🌾  
**Period:** 1865–1895  
**One-line:** Literature that focuses on the specific culture, dialect, and landscape of a particular geographic region of America.

**Core beliefs:**
- Local color and specific place matter — not just a generic "America"
- Dialect, local customs, and regional characters are worth serious literary attention
- Often a response to industrialization and urbanization — preserve what is disappearing

**Key characteristics (how to spot it):**
- Strong sense of place (a specific state, region, community)
- Local dialect in dialogue
- Characters who are tied to their land or community
- Often nostalgic or preservationist in tone

**Works in this course:**
- Mark Twain's "A True Story" (Week 5 — context only, not an exam focus work)

**Exam context:**
- Regionalism is less likely to appear as a heavy exam focus given the weeks covered
- Know it for MCQ matching — if asked "which school focuses on local color and dialect?" → Regionalism
- Know Twain is associated with it

### Term Bank

**LITERARY DEVICES**

| Term | Definition | Example from course |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Dramatic monologue | One character speaks directly to a silent listener or the reader, revealing their personality | Doc Hill and Abel Melveny (Spoon River Anthology) |
| Dramatic irony | The reader knows something the characters do not | Richard Cory — we sense the ending before the townspeople do |
| Volta | A turn or shift in a poem's direction, tone, or argument | Richard Cory — the entire poem turns in the last two lines |
| Foreshadowing | Hints at what will happen later | The old-timer's warning in "To Build a Fire" |
| Personification | Giving human qualities to non-human things | The sea in "The Open Boat" is described almost as if it has intention |
| Symbolism | An object or element that represents a larger idea | The dog in "To Build a Fire" (instinct/natural law), the mill in "The Mill" (obsolescence) |
| Free verse | Poetry with no fixed rhyme or meter | Masters' Spoon River poems |
| Understatement | Describing something as less significant than it is, for effect | The suicide in "Richard Cory" is described in one flat line |
| Irony | A contrast between expectation and reality | The most capable man (Billie) dies in "The Open Boat" |
| Narrative ellipsis | Leaving out key moments — the reader fills in the gaps | The wife's death in "The Mill" is never stated |
| First-person plural narrator | Narrated by "we" — a collective voice | "Richard Cory" — the townspeople narrate as a group |
| Catalogue / Listing | A long list used for rhetorical or emotional effect | Abel Melveny lists his machines — the list becomes sad and absurd |
| Epitaph form | A poem written as if it were a gravestone inscription | All Spoon River Anthology poems |
| Iambic tetrameter | A rhythmic pattern of alternating unstressed/stressed syllables, four per line | "Richard Cory" uses this — more traditional than Masters |

**CONCEPTS**

| Term | Definition | Example from course |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Determinism | The idea that all events, including human actions, are determined by forces outside individual control | "To Build a Fire" — the man's death was inevitable given the conditions |
| Fatalism | The belief that all events are fixed and resistance is pointless | "To Build a Fire" — no matter what the man tries, he cannot survive |
| Hubris | Excessive pride or confidence, especially when it leads to downfall | The man in "To Build a Fire" refuses to believe the cold can kill him |
| Indifference of Nature | The idea that the natural world has no concern for human life or suffering | Central theme in both "To Build a Fire" and "The Open Boat" |
| Materialism | Placing excessive value on physical possessions and wealth | Abel Melveny — he collected machines but never used them |
| Obsolescence | When something becomes outdated and no longer useful | The miller in "The Mill" — his trade is replaced by industrial machines |
| Procrastination | Delaying action despite knowing what needs to be done | Abel Melveny's core flaw — always planning, never doing |
| Instinct vs Intellect | The conflict between animal intuition and human reasoning | Dog vs man in "To Build a Fire" — instinct wins |
| Subjective perception | The idea that reality is filtered through an individual's senses and emotions | Impressionism in "The Open Boat" — we see the sea through the correspondent's fear |
