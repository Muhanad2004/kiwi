## Authors Quick Reference

**Jack London (1876–1916)**
- American Naturalist writer
- Known for wilderness survival narratives
- Influenced by Darwin and Spencer's social Darwinism
- "To Build a Fire" is his most anthologized short story

**Stephen Crane (1871–1900)**
- American Naturalist AND Impressionist
- "The Open Boat" based on his real experience surviving a shipwreck in 1897
- Style: vivid sensory detail, psychological depth, irony
- Other famous work: "The Red Badge of Courage"

**Edgar Lee Masters (1868–1950)**
- American poet, Spoon River Anthology (1915)
- Each poem = one dead resident of fictional Spoon River, speaking from the grave
- Modernist free verse, Realist subject matter
- Themes: small-town life, regret, community, mortality

**Edwin Arlington Robinson (1869–1935)**
- American poet, early Modernist
- Famous for short dramatic portraits of individuals
- Won Pulitzer Prize three times
- Style: controlled, ironic, emotionally restrained
- Themes: failure, isolation, disillusionment, industrial America

## Literary Schools — Exam-Ready Definitions

**Naturalism**
Naturalism treats human beings as biological creatures shaped by their heredity and environment. In Naturalist fiction, characters do not have meaningful free will — their fates are determined by forces larger than themselves. Nature in Naturalist works is not evil or cruel. It is simply indifferent. The natural world operates by its own laws and has no interest in human survival or suffering.
Key terms: indifference, determinism, environment, survival, instinct, biological forces.

**Impressionism**
Impressionism in literature focuses on subjective perception — how reality appears to a character in a given emotional and sensory state. Instead of an objective description of what is happening, the reader experiences events through the shifting mood, feeling, and sensation of the character. This is why two characters can experience the same storm very differently. There is no fixed, universal truth in Impressionism — only the truth of the perceiving mind.
Key terms: perception, sensation, emotion, subjectivity, mood, fleeting impression.

**Realism**
Realism emerged after the Civil War as a reaction to Romanticism. Realist writers depicted ordinary people in ordinary situations — no heroic plots, no divine intervention, no idealization. The goal was to represent life as it actually was: complicated, unglamorous, shaped by social and economic forces. Realism includes sharp observation of class, race, gender, and regional culture.
Key terms: ordinary life, social conditions, accuracy, anti-romantic, psychological depth.

**Modernism**
Modernism arose in the early twentieth century, shaped by WWI, industrialization, and the collapse of traditional certainties. Modernist writers rejected the formal conventions of earlier literature — fixed rhyme schemes, omniscient narrators, heroic plots — in favor of fragmentation, ambiguity, free verse, and stream of consciousness. Modernist literature often explores alienation, isolation, and the impossibility of making meaning in a broken world.
Key terms: alienation, fragmentation, disillusionment, free verse, dramatic monologue, anti-heroic.

**Determinism**
Determinism is the philosophical belief that all events — including human choices — are the inevitable result of prior causes. In literature, Determinist fiction shows characters whose fates are sealed by forces outside their control: biology, environment, social class, heredity. The man in "To Build a Fire" could not have survived because the conditions made survival impossible — this is Determinism in action.
Key terms: inevitable, fate, no free will, prior causes, environment as destiny.

**Fatalism**
Closely related to Determinism. Fatalism is the belief that resistance to fate is useless — what will happen will happen, and nothing can prevent it. In "To Build a Fire," the man's every attempt to survive fails. Not because he makes the wrong choices, but because the outcome was fixed.

## Key Thematic Connections Across Works

**Indifference of Nature:**
Both "To Build a Fire" and "The Open Boat" share this as their central theme. In London's story, the cold does not target the man — it simply operates by physical law. In Crane's story, the sea is not malicious — it just does not notice the men. This is the Naturalist core.

**The Best Man Dies:**
In "The Open Boat," Billie the oiler — the most hardworking and capable of the four men — drowns in the surf while others survive. This is Crane's darkest argument: nature does not reward virtue or effort.

**Instinct beats intellect:**
"To Build a Fire" — the dog's instinct saves it while the man's intellect kills him. The man knows what to do but his knowledge is useless against conditions he cannot control.

**Appearances vs Reality:**
"Richard Cory" — the townspeople see a perfect man; the reality is total despair. "The Mill" — the miller's wife goes through domestic routines while her world has collapsed. Both Robinson poems subvert surface appearance.

**Doc Hill vs Abel Melveny:**
Two men, same form (Spoon River dramatic monologue), opposite lives. Doc Hill gave everything and died with dignity. Abel Melveny kept everything and died with regret. Masters uses this contrast to argue for action over accumulation.

**Industrial obsolescence:**
"The Mill" and "Abel Melveny" both reflect the anxiety of the Second Industrial Revolution — when machines replaced human trades and skills. The miller loses his livelihood. Abel fills his life with machines but creates nothing. The industrial age is a destructive force in both poems.

## Poem Line-by-Line Notes (exam risk: excerpt with "what does this line mean")

### Richard Cory — Key Lines

"And he was always quietly arrayed" → He dressed well but without showiness — elegance without arrogance
"And he was always human when he talked" → He was not condescending despite his status — he spoke to everyone as an equal
"But still he fluttered pulses when he said, 'Good-morning'" → Even a casual greeting from him was exciting to the townspeople
"And he was rich — yes, richer than a king" → Hyperbole — his wealth was beyond ordinary comprehension
"And admirably schooled in every grace" → He was educated, refined, and polished in every social manner
"In fine, we thought that he was everything" → The townspeople's summary: he had it all
"To make us wish that we were in his place" → Pure envy — they wanted his life
"And went without the meat, and cursed the bread" → The townspeople were poor and struggling — survival-level poverty
"And Richard Cory, one calm summer night, went home and put a bullet through his head" → The volta — devastatingly flat and casual phrasing for an act of suicide

### The Mill — Key Lines

"The miller's wife had waited long" → Immediately sets up waiting, absence, dread
"The tea was cold, the fire was dead" → Domestic details that signal something is very wrong
"And there was nothing in the sound / To stir her hand or lift her eyes" → She has gone numb — the shock of what she knows
"She thought of it the long day through" → The miller's wife cannot stop thinking about death
"And, if her heart had found a way / To tell what she had found below" → She found her husband's body in the mill — described obliquely
"Black water, smooth above the weir" → The pond near the mill — foreshadowing her own death by drowning
"Like a stream running through a dream" → The water imagery — she is drawn to it, almost hypnotically
The ending lines describe the water and her going to it — the implication is that she drowns herself

### Doc Hill — Key Lines

Doctor speaks about tending to the poor, often without pay.
His voice is quiet and factual — no self-pity, no resentment.
He treated people regardless of their ability to pay because it was right.
The poem closes without resolution or triumph — just the fact of a life lived decently.

### Abel Melveny — Key Lines

Abel lists machine after machine that he bought.
The tone is almost excited at first — the accumulation was thrilling.
Then the regret enters: he never used them, he never did what he planned.
The machines rust in his yard — symbols of what he could have built but did not.
The poem ends with the feeling that he wasted the only life he had.

## Essay Prep — "To Build a Fire" Claim Bank

Use any of these as a thesis for the exam essay:

1. In "To Build a Fire," Jack London uses Naturalism and Determinism to show that nature is completely indifferent to human survival, and that the forces of environment will always overpower human intelligence.

2. Through the contrast between the man and the dog, London argues in "To Build a Fire" that instinct — not intellect — is the only reliable guide in a Naturalist world where environment determines fate.

3. Jack London's "To Build a Fire" demonstrates the Naturalist belief that human beings are powerless against the forces of the natural world, because the cold operates as a force of Determinism that the man's choices cannot overcome.

4. In "To Build a Fire," London shows that hubris — specifically the man's overconfidence in his own intelligence — is what makes his Naturalistic fate complete, because he refuses to take the warnings of nature or the old-timer seriously.

## Confusable Works — Study Alert

These are pairs students commonly mix up. Know the difference cold.

| Pair | Key difference |
|------|----------------|
| Doc Hill vs Abel Melveny | Doc Hill = active, selfless, dignified. Abel = passive, selfish, regretful |
| Richard Cory vs The Mill | Cory = shocking sudden ending, outsider narration. Mill = slow dread, domestic intimacy |
| To Build a Fire vs The Open Boat | London = one man, wilderness, cold. Crane = four men, sea, lifeboat |
| Naturalism vs Determinism | Naturalism = school of thought about nature and biology. Determinism = philosophical concept about inevitable fate. They often appear together |
| Naturalism vs Impressionism | Naturalism = external forces on humans. Impressionism = internal perception of those forces |
