## WORK 1: To Build a Fire (Jack London, 1908)

**Literary Schools:** Naturalism, Determinism  
**Visual Identity:** Cold, stark, high contrast. Deep hunter green on near-white. Sparse layout. Lots of whitespace to feel like snow and isolation.  
**Accent treatment:** Hunter green headers, yellow-green highlight on key terms, thin horizontal rules between sections.

### Hero Block
```
🔥 To Build a Fire
Jack London · 1908 · Short Story

[Naturalism] [Determinism] [Fatalism]

"A man without a imagination..."
— The narrator's observation on the protagonist's fatal flaw
```

### Summary
```
❄️ A nameless man travels alone through the Yukon wilderness in brutal cold, ignoring 
the warnings of an experienced old-timer. He has a dog with him, but the dog knows 
instinctively that the conditions are dangerous — the man does not.

🌡️ After his fire goes out and his hands become too numb to work, the man realizes 
he cannot survive. He tries everything to warm himself but fails.

🐺 The dog stays alive because it follows its instincts. The man dies because he 
trusted his logic over nature's power. Nature did not care either way.
```

### Themes
```
🌿 Nature's Indifference      Nature has no interest in whether the man lives or dies
⚙️ Instinct vs Intellect      The dog survives because it listens to instinct — the man doesn't
🎲 Determinism                 The man's fate was sealed by forces outside his control
🧠 Hubris                      The man's overconfidence in himself is what kills him
🏔️ Human Insignificance       Against the Yukon, one man means nothing
```

### Literary Devices
```
Third-person limited narrator   Keeps us close to the man's thoughts so we feel his false confidence
Foreshadowing                   The old-timer's warning predicts exactly what happens
Naturalistic description        Cold is described in precise, scientific, unfeeling detail
Symbolism — the dog             The dog represents pure instinct and natural law
Irony                           The man is intelligent but dies; the dog is "just an animal" but survives
```

### Literary School Connection
- **Naturalism:** humans are controlled by environment and biological forces, not free will
- **Determinism:** the man's death was inevitable given the conditions — no choice could have saved him
- **Fatalism:** the story implies resistance is pointless against natural forces

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 The essay will very likely be on this work
→ Be ready to write about Naturalism AND Determinism together — link both to specific moments
→ Know the dog vs man contrast cold (pun intended)
→ Know the old-timer's warning and what it represents
→ Be ready to identify an excerpt from this story
→ Key quote zone: the man's thoughts about his own body failing
→ Possible MCQ: "Which work best illustrates Determinism?" → To Build a Fire
```

## WORK 2: The Open Boat (Stephen Crane, 1897)

**Literary Schools:** Naturalism, Impressionism  
**Visual Identity:** Moody ocean feel. Sage green dominant, wavy subtle CSS border treatment on cards. Slightly more layered layout — impressionism means layered perception.  
**Accent treatment:** Sage green headers, champagne card backgrounds with sage border, brick red for danger/indifference quotes.

### Hero Block
```
🌊 The Open Boat
Stephen Crane · 1897 · Short Story

[Naturalism] [Impressionism]

"None of them knew the colour of the sky."
— Opening line, immediately placing us inside limited human perception
```

### Summary
```
🚢 Four men — a cook, an oiler, a correspondent, and the captain — are stranded in 
a tiny lifeboat after their ship sinks. They can see shore but cannot reach it because 
of the dangerous surf.

🌊 The sea is huge and the boat is tiny. The men row and bail and take turns sleeping, 
slowly exhausting themselves while wondering if anyone on shore can see them.

😔 The oiler (Billie), who worked the hardest and was the most capable, drowns in the 
surf when they finally attempt to reach shore. The others survive. Nature rewards no one.
```

### Themes
```
🌊 Nature's Indifference      The sea is not cruel — it simply does not notice the men
🤝 Brotherhood/Solidarity     The men form a deep bond because survival forces cooperation
💀 Mortality                   Every moment could be their last — they feel this constantly
👁️ Perception vs Reality      Impressionism: we only see what the men see, not objective truth
🎲 Fate and Helplessness      The oiler's death is cruel and random — the best man dies
```

### Literary Devices
```
Impressionism                   We experience the story through the characters' shifting emotions and senses
Point of view — 3rd limited     Focused on the correspondent's perception specifically
Irony                           The most physically capable man (Billie) dies; weaker men survive
Repetition                      "If I am going to be drowned" — the correspondent's recurring thought
Personification                 The sea and waves are described almost as forces with intention
Naturalistic description        The men are tiny creatures in a vast, mechanical, indifferent world
```

### Literary School Connection
- **Naturalism:** the oiler's death proves humans are powerless against natural forces
- **Impressionism:** the story is told through feeling, perception, and shifting emotional states rather than objective narration

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 Model paragraph in your notes uses this story — know the structure
→ Be ready to link Naturalism AND Impressionism in one answer (just like the model paragraph)
→ The opening line ("None of them knew the colour of the sky") — know why it matters (Impressionism)
→ Know why Billie the oiler's death is significant — it proves nature is indifferent
→ Possible MCQ: "Which device does Crane use to show limited perception?" → Impressionism
→ Quote ID risk: the "throw bricks at the temple" passage
```

## WORK 3: Doc Hill (Edgar Lee Masters, 1915)

**Literary Schools:** Modernism, Realism  
**Visual Identity:** Clean, typographic, editorial. Think a newspaper obituary column. Lots of text treatment, minimal decoration. Black on cream with yellow-green small accents.  
**Accent treatment:** Dark text dominant, yellow-green used as ink-underline on key lines, Lora serif for the poem text itself.

### Hero Block
```
🪦 Doc Hill
Edgar Lee Masters · 1915 · Poem (Spoon River Anthology)

[Modernism] [Realism]

"I went up and down the streets here..."
— The speaker reflects on a life of service from beyond the grave
```

### Summary
```
👴 Doc Hill speaks from the grave (a key Spoon River Anthology device — all speakers are dead).
He tells us about his life as a doctor in a small town, caring for the poor without charging them.

💊 He worked constantly, often for free, because he genuinely cared about the people in his 
community. The town respected him but may not have fully understood his sacrifice.

🪦 In death, he seems at peace. His life had meaning because of what he gave, not what he gained.
The poem is quiet, reflective, and anti-heroic in a Modernist way.
```

### Themes
```
🩺 Service and Sacrifice      Doc Hill gave everything to his community with nothing in return
🪦 Death and Reflection       Speaking from the grave forces an honest retrospective
🏘️ Small Town Life            Spoon River is a fictional small American town — representative of any town
💰 Class and Poverty          He treated the poor for free — a quiet political statement
🕊️ Quiet Dignity              No drama, no heroism — just a decent life, which is enough
```

### Literary Devices
```
Dramatic monologue             The dead speaker addresses the reader directly from the grave
Free verse                     No rhyme scheme — Modernist rejection of traditional poetic form
Epitaph form                   Each Spoon River poem reads like a gravestone speech
Understatement                 Doc Hill's sacrifice is described simply, without sentimentality
Realism                        Ordinary life, ordinary people — no romanticization
```

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 Know this poem line by line — she may give an excerpt and ask who is speaking or what it means
→ Know the Spoon River Anthology concept: dead speakers, dramatic monologue form
→ Know the difference between Doc Hill and Abel Melveny (same collection, different character types)
→ Possible MCQ: "Which literary school does Spoon River Anthology belong to?" → Modernism/Realism
→ Possible short answer: explain the significance of the "speaking from the grave" device
```

## WORK 4: Abel Melveny (Edgar Lee Masters, 1915)

**Literary Schools:** Modernism, Realism  
**Visual Identity:** Cluttered, regretful feel — slight diagonal card layout, slightly warmer tones. This is a more bitter poem than Doc Hill.  
**Accent treatment:** Brick red used more here — regret, bitterness. Same Lora serif for poem text.

### Hero Block
```
🔧 Abel Melveny
Edgar Lee Masters · 1915 · Poem (Spoon River Anthology)

[Modernism] [Realism]

"I bought every kind of machine that's known..."
— Abel speaks of accumulation and the emptiness it left
```

### Summary
```
🔧 Abel Melveny also speaks from the grave. He spent his life collecting tools and machines 
— buying every gadget and device he could find — but never actually used them.

😔 He was always planning to do something, to build something, to start something. But the 
plans never became action. He died with a yard full of rusting machines and an empty life.

⚙️ The poem is a critique of materialism and procrastination. Abel had potential but wasted it 
on accumulation instead of creation.
```

### Themes
```
⚙️ Materialism                 Collecting things is not the same as living a meaningful life
😔 Regret and Wasted Potential  Abel realizes too late that he never did what he planned
🔄 Procrastination              Always preparing, never doing — a universal human failure
🪦 Death as Revelation         Only in death does Abel see clearly what he wasted
🏭 Industrial America          The machines symbolize the Gilded Age obsession with technology and progress
```

### Literary Devices
```
Dramatic monologue             Same form as Doc Hill — dead speaker addresses reader
Catalogue/Listing              Abel lists his machines — the list itself becomes absurd and sad
Irony                          A man surrounded by tools of creation created nothing
Symbolism — machines           Represent the unfulfilled potential of a life unlived
Free verse                     Modernist form — no forced rhyme or meter
```

### Contrast with Doc Hill
```
Doc Hill gave everything → died with dignity
Abel Melveny kept everything → died with regret

Doc Hill: active, selfless
Abel Melveny: passive, self-absorbed

She may ask you to compare these two directly.
```

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 The Doc Hill vs Abel Melveny contrast is a likely short answer or MCQ link
→ Know the Spoon River Anthology context for both
→ Be ready to identify an excerpt as either Doc Hill or Abel Melveny
→ Abel = regret, materialism, procrastination
→ Doc Hill = service, sacrifice, quiet dignity
```

## WORK 5: Richard Cory (Edwin Arlington Robinson, 1897)

**Literary Schools:** Modernism, Realism  
**Visual Identity:** Elegant then shattered. Start the card clean and pristine (champagne, gold accents), then use a subtle visual break or crack effect via CSS border treatment. The poem is about facade.  
**Accent treatment:** Yellow-green for the "golden" surface image of Richard Cory, brick red for the shocking ending.

### Hero Block
```
👑 Richard Cory
Edwin Arlington Robinson · 1897 · Poem

[Modernism] [Realism]

"And Richard Cory, one calm summer night, went home and put a bullet through his head."
— The final lines, completely subverting everything before them
```

### Summary
```
👑 The townspeople describe Richard Cory as perfect. He is rich, educated, graceful, 
and admired by everyone. People watch him walk and wish they were him.

😶 The townspeople themselves are poor and struggling, going without meat and working hard.
They envy Richard Cory because he seems to have everything.

💥 In the final two lines, Richard Cory goes home one night and shoots himself. No explanation 
is given. The contrast between his image and his inner life is total and devastating.
```

### Themes
```
💔 Appearances vs Reality      What people project is not what they feel inside
💰 Wealth and Unhappiness      Money and status do not equal fulfillment or happiness
👥 Class and Envy              The working class envies Cory — they are wrong to
🧠 Isolation                   Cory is surrounded by admirers but completely alone
😶 Narrative Perspective       The poem is told by the townspeople — they never really knew him
```

### Literary Devices
```
Dramatic irony                 We (and the town) build up Richard Cory, making the ending more shocking
Volta (turn)                   The poem pivots completely in the last two lines
First-person plural narrator   "We" — the collective voice of the working class townspeople
Understatement                 The suicide is described in one flat, casual line — devastating because of it
Contrast                       His elegant image vs the brutal reality of his death
Iambic tetrameter              More traditional form than Masters — note the difference
```

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 High risk for excerpt + "which work is this from" — the opening and closing lines are very distinct
→ Know who narrates (the townspeople as a collective "we")
→ The contrast between class envy and Richard Cory's actual suffering is key
→ Possible essay prompt: how does Robinson use narrative perspective to create irony?
→ Possible MCQ: "What literary device does the ending of Richard Cory demonstrate?" → Volta / Dramatic irony
```

## WORK 6: The Mill (Edwin Arlington Robinson, 1920)

**Literary Schools:** Modernism, Realism  
**Visual Identity:** Dark, waterlogged, heavy. Deeper sage and hunter green tones. A feeling of quiet tragedy and industrial decline.  
**Accent treatment:** Darker palette than Richard Cory — this poem is grimmer and less surprising, more slowly sad.

### Hero Block
```
⚙️ The Mill
Edwin Arlington Robinson · 1920 · Poem

[Modernism] [Realism] [Second Industrial Revolution context]

"The miller's wife had waited long..."
— A poem of quiet domestic tragedy and industrial despair
```

### Summary
```
⚙️ The miller's wife waits for her husband to come home. He does not come. She eventually 
finds him dead — he has hanged himself in the mill.

🌊 The poem ends with the miller's wife also dying — implied drowning in the pond near the mill.
She follows her husband into death, seemingly because the world they knew is gone.

🏭 The backdrop is the Second Industrial Revolution — mills are being replaced by machines,
and the miller's trade is obsolete. The poem links personal tragedy to economic and social change.
```

### Themes
```
🏭 Industrial Decline          The end of the miller's trade represents the end of a way of life
💔 Grief and Despair           The wife's implied suicide mirrors her husband's — grief is total
⏳ Obsolescence                Being replaced by machines destroys identity and purpose
🌊 Death as Escape             Both characters choose death rather than face a changed world
😶 Silence and Implication     The wife's death is never stated directly — the reader understands it
```

### Literary Devices
```
Implication/Suggestion         The wife's death is never stated — the reader infers it from the imagery
Symbolism — the pond           Water as death, the same way the mill represents their lost livelihood
Dramatic irony                 The reader understands the wife's fate before she perhaps does
Modernist restraint            Emotion is held back — the tragedy is told quietly, which amplifies it
Narrative ellipsis             Key moments are left out — we fill in the gaps
```

### Contrast with Richard Cory
```
Richard Cory: sudden shock ending, external observer narration
The Mill: slow, building dread, more interior and domestic
Both: suicide, despair beneath a surface, Modernist understatement
Both: from the same poet — Robinson's voice is consistent (controlled, ironic, restrained)
```

### Exam Tips
```
🎯 She said to read the poems carefully and know what each LINE means
→ Know the mill as a symbol of industrial obsolescence
→ Know that the wife's death is implied, not stated — and why that matters literarily
→ Be ready to identify the poem from an excerpt
→ Possible short answer: explain the significance of the mill in the context of the Second Industrial Revolution
→ Compare/contrast Richard Cory and The Mill is a realistic exam question
```
